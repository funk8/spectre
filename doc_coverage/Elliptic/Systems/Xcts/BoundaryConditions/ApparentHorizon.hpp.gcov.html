     258             :  * \brief Impose the surface is a quasi-equilibrium apparent horizon.
     259             :  *
     260             :  * These boundary conditions on the conformal factor \f$\psi\f$, the lapse
     261             :  * \f$\alpha\f$ and the shift \f$\beta^i\f$ impose the surface is an apparent
     262             :  * horizon, i.e. that the expansion on the surface vanishes: \f$\Theta=0\f$.
     263             :  * Specifically, we impose:
     264             :  *
     265             :  * \f{align}
     266             :  * \label{eq:ah_psi}
     267             :  * \bar{s}^k\bar{D}_k\psi &= -\frac{\psi^3}{8\alpha}\bar{s}_i\bar{s}_j\left(
     268             :  * (\bar{L}\beta)^{ij} - \bar{u}^{ij}\right)
     269             :  * - \frac{\psi}{4}\bar{m}^{ij}\bar{\nabla}_i\bar{s}_j + \frac{1}{6}K\psi^3
     270             :  * \\
     271             :  * \label{eq:ah_beta}
     272             :  * \beta_\mathrm{excess}^i &= \frac{\alpha}{\psi^2}\bar{s}^i
     273             :  * + \epsilon_{ijk}\Omega^j x^k
     274             :  * \f}
     275             :  *
     276             :  * following section 7.2 of \cite Pfeiffer2005zm, section 12.3.2 of
     277             :  * \cite BaumgarteShapiro or section II.B.1 of \cite Varma2018sqd. In these
     278             :  * equations \f$\bar{s}_i\f$ is the conformal surface normal to the apparent
     279             :  * horizon, \f$\bar{m}^{ij}=\bar{\gamma}^{ij}-\bar{s}^i\bar{s}^j\f$ is the
     280             :  * induced conformal surface metric (denoted \f$\tilde{h}^{ij}\f$ in
     281             :  * \cite Pfeiffer2005zm and \cite Varma2018sqd) and \f$\bar{D}\f$ is the
     282             :  * covariant derivative w.r.t. to the conformal metric \f$\bar{\gamma}_{ij}\f$.
     283             :  * Note that e.g. in \cite Varma2018sqd Eq. (16) appears the surface-normal
     284             :  * \f$s^i\f$, not the _conformal_ surface normal \f$\bar{s}^i = \psi^2 s^i\f$.
     285             :  * To incur a spin on the apparent horizon we can freely choose the rotational
     286             :  * parameters \f$\boldsymbol{\Omega}\f$. Note that for a Kerr solution with
     287             :  * dimensionless spin \f$\boldsymbol{\chi}\f$ the rotational parameters at the
     288             :  * outer horizon are \f$\boldsymbol{\Omega} =
     289             :  * -\frac{\boldsymbol{\chi}}{2r_+}\f$, where \f$r_+ / M = 1 + \sqrt{1 -
     290             :  * \chi^2}\f$ (see e.g. Eq. (8) in \cite Ossokine2015yla). \f$\epsilon_{ijk}\f$
     291             :  * is the flat-space Levi-Civita symbol.
     292             :  *
     293             :  * Note that the quasi-equilibrium conditions don't restrict the boundary
     294             :  * condition for the lapse. The choice for the lapse boundary condition is made
     295             :  * by the `Lapse` input-file options. Currently implemented choices are:
     296             :  * - A zero von-Neumann boundary condition:
     297             :  *   \f$\bar{s}^k\bar{D}_k(\alpha\psi) = 0\f$
     298             :  * - A Dirichlet boundary condition imposing the lapse of a Kerr-Schild analytic
     299             :  *   solution.
     300             :  *
     301             :  * \par Negative-expansion boundary conditions:
     302             :  * This class also supports negative-expansion boundary conditions following
     303             :  * section II.B.2 of \cite Varma2018sqd by taking a Kerr solution as additional
     304             :  * parameter and computing its expansion at the excision surface. Choosing an
     305             :  * excision surface _within_ the apparent horizon of the Kerr solution will
     306             :  * result in a negative expansion that is added to the boundary condition for
     307             :  * the conformal factor. Therefore, the excision surface will lie within an
     308             :  * apparent horizon. Specifically, we add the quantity
     309             :  * \f$\frac{\psi^3}{4}\Theta_\mathrm{Kerr}\f$ to (\f$\ref{eq:ah_psi}\f$), where
     310             :  * \f$\Theta_\mathrm{Kerr}\f$ is the expansion of the specified Kerr solution at
     311             :  * the excision surface, and we add the quantity \f$\epsilon = s_i
     312             :  * \beta_\mathrm{Kerr}^i - \alpha_\mathrm{Kerr}\f$ to the orthogonal part
     313             :  * \f$s_i\beta_\mathrm{excess}^i\f$ of (\f$\ref{eq:ah_beta}\f$).
     314             :  *
     315             :  * \note When negative-expansion boundary conditions are selected, the Kerr
     316             :  * solution gets evaluated at every boundary-condition application. This may
     317             :  * turn out to incur a significant computational cost, in which case a future
     318             :  * optimization might be to pre-compute the negative-expansion quantities at
